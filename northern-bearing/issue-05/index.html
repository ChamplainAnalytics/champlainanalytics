01 ◆ Section 01 — Macro Signals

Canada's Labour Market Just Changed What June 10 Means. US CPI This Morning Changes Everything Else.

◆ StatCan Labour Force Survey — April 2026

Canada Lost 18,000 Jobs in April. Unemployment Hit 6.9%. The Hike Scenario Got Harder to Tell.

Statistics Canada's April Labour Force Survey, released May 8, recorded a net loss of 18,000 jobs — the first monthly miss in the jobs numbers in several months, and directly against economist consensus for a gain. The unemployment rate rose 0.2 percentage points to 6.9%, its highest level in six months, driven by 51,000 more Canadians entering the job search pool rather than a surge in layoffs. Full-time employment declined 47,000 in the month. Over the first four months of 2026, full-time positions have now fallen by 111,000. The miss changes the conversation heading into the Bank of Canada's June 10 decision. Macklem's April 29 hike language was explicitly conditional on energy inflation spreading into broader goods and services prices. A deteriorating labour market makes that spillover harder to argue — and harder to justify tightening into.

◆ Bank of Canada — Context Post-LFS

RBC Economics: The BoC's Hike Language Was Conditional. The Conditions Are Getting Harder to Meet.

RBC Economics confirmed after the April 29 BoC decision that Governor Macklem's hike language was contingent, not directional: the Bank was not forecasting a hike but rather acknowledging the risk if energy prices remained elevated AND fed through to persistent broader inflation. To date, core inflation measures have slowed. Longer-term inflation expectations remain anchored. With WTI now at $95 — down from $101 a week ago — and April employment missing, the conditional threshold for a hike becomes harder for the Governing Council to clear before June 10. The BoC Summary of Deliberations, publishing Wednesday May 13 at 1:30 PM ET, will reveal how much internal debate the hike language actually represented.

◆ US Employment — April 2026

US Added 115,000 Jobs in April. Double the Forecast. But the Detail Was Weaker Than the Headline.

April US nonfarm payrolls rose by 115,000, beating the Dow Jones consensus of 55,000. The unemployment rate held at 4.3%. Average hourly earnings grew only 0.2% month-over-month (3.6% YoY), below the 0.3% estimate — the softest wage print in months. Underneath the headline: labour force participation fell to 61.8%, the lowest since October 2021; information sector employment is down 342,000 (11%) since November 2022, aligned with AI-driven job displacement; healthcare alone accounts for nearly all net job creation over the past twelve months, while every other sector combined shed positions. The labour market has not collapsed. It has split. And the soft wage print matters more to the Fed's inflation calculus than the headline job number.

06 ◆ Section 06 — Currency & Global Markets

Oil Slides, Jobs Disappoint, and the Loonie Faces a Two-Force Tug-of-War

◆ CAD/USD — Week of May 4-8

The Loonie: Caught Between Two Opposing Forces This Week

The Canadian dollar faced competing pressures last week. Oil's 7% decline is normally loonie-negative — Canada's currency moves with crude because higher oil prices improve the terms of trade and increase demand for CAD. But the jobs miss simultaneously reduced the probability of a BoC hike — which is also loonie-negative. The net effect was a CAD under modest pressure, with USD/CAD moving back above 1.36. The loonie's near-term trajectory depends on two factors: today's US CPI (which moves the USD side of the pair) and whether WTI stabilizes or continues declining. A hot CPI strengthens the USD broadly, pushing USD/CAD higher (weaker CAD). A benign CPI allows the loonie to recover the oil-driven losses.

◆ Impact on VFV / US Equity Holders

USD Strength on Hot CPI Would Offset US Equity Gains in CAD Terms. Here Is the Mechanic.

If today's April CPI prints hot and the US dollar strengthens as a result, VFV.TO holders get a dual benefit: the S&P 500 in USD terms is unaffected, and a stronger USD means their USD-denominated holdings are worth more in CAD. The flip side applies to a benign CPI: USD weakness means the CAD-equivalent value of VFV falls even if the S&P 500 is flat or rising. Canadian investors holding US equity through CAD-listed, unhedged ETFs are effectively running two positions simultaneously — a bet on US equities and an implicit short on CAD. This week makes both sides visible.

◆ Iran / Strait of Hormuz — Latest

US Forces Passed Through Hormuz, Clashed With Iran, and Declared a Ceasefire Is Still In Effect. The Oil Market Has No Idea What to Believe.

Last week's oil price action tells the story of Hormuz uncertainty precisely: WTI fell more than 7% but closed the week near $95 after fresh clashes between US and Iranian forces threatened to derail peace talks. US Central Command reported American destroyers passed through the strait; Iran accused the US of violating the ceasefire; Trump said the ceasefire remained in effect despite the exchanges. The IEA confirmed the conflict is removing approximately 14 million barrels per day from global supply. The market is attempting to price two simultaneous and contradictory outcomes — ceasefire and escalation — at once. Until one resolves, volatility will remain high and the WCS-WTI differential will compress Canadian producers' realized prices further.

◆ Global Markets — EU, Asia

Oil-Importing Economies Are Feeling the Squeeze. Canada Remains the G7's Outlier.

European and Asian economies — net oil importers — continue to absorb the inflationary impact of $90-95 WTI with less structural buffer than Canada. The IMF's April 2026 World Economic Outlook projects global GDP growth falling from a pre-conflict 3.4% baseline to 3.1% in 2026 under a moderate conflict scenario. Canada, as a net energy exporter with the lowest US tariff rate among major trading partners at 5.2%, sits structurally apart from this pattern. The Spring Economic Update confirmed Canada as a projected second-fastest G7 GDP grower in 2026-27. That structural advantage does not insulate Canada from the weak labour data — but it does mean the damage from global headwinds arrives more slowly than elsewhere.

SO WHAT — FOR THE SELF-DIRECTED INVESTOR

The loonie's two-force problem this week is actually clarifying: it removes the ability to treat currency moves as a simple directional bet and forces investors to focus on the underlying equity positions. For holders of VFV.TO in an RRSP, the currency math is a secondary consideration to the S&P 500's direction — and the S&P 500's direction this week is today's CPI. For holders of XIU or XIC, the CAD dimension is largely irrelevant as you are holding CAD-denominated assets with Canadian revenues. The review that matters most is still account placement: US-listed equities belong in the RRSP for the withholding tax exemption, and this week's volatility in CAD/USD makes that efficiency even more visible.

Warsh Inherits a Divided Committee, a Hot Inflation Print, and No Mandate for Cuts or Hikes. That Is the Problem.

Kevin Warsh's Senate confirmation vote is expected this week, placing him in the chair by Friday May 15 — the day Powell's term expires. Warsh enters under conditions that make his stated agenda difficult to execute immediately. US core inflation is at 3.2% (March). Today's April headline CPI is expected at 3.7-3.8%. The FOMC voted 8-4 at its last session — the most dissents since 1992. Bank of America has abandoned its 2026 rate cut forecast entirely, pushing the first cut to H2 2027. Natixis CIB's chief US economist described Warsh as likely "the least influential Fed chair in a long time" given the committee's resistance to rapid policy change. His first test is the June 16-17 FOMC meeting, which will include updated economic projections — and may be the last dot plot ever published if Warsh follows through on his plan to eliminate forward guidance.

◆ Institutional Bond Market

Bank of America Has Abandoned 2026 Rate Cuts. That Has Direct Implications for Canadian Fixed Income Positioning.

Bank of America's latest forecast revision removes all 2026 Fed rate cut expectations, pushing the first cut to H2 2027. JPMorgan scenarios project inflation remaining above 2% through early 2027. For Canadian institutional investors with exposure to US Treasuries, this shifts the duration calculus: if US yields hold at 4%+ for another 18 months, longer-duration bond holdings underperform. The implication flows through to the Canadian market: if the Fed holds for longer, the Bank of Canada has less room to cut even if domestic conditions warrant it — because cutting too far below the Fed creates CAD weakness and imported inflation. The rate differential between BoC (2.25%) and the Fed (3.5%-3.75%) is already wide. Widening it further is a loonie problem.

◆ CPP Investments — Energy Positioning

The Maple 8 Were Long Energy at $100 Oil. At $95, the Thesis Does Not Break. At $80, It Gets Tested.

CPP Investments ($714.4B AUM) and the broader Maple 8 pension complex have structural exposure to Canadian energy through direct infrastructure holdings, pipeline positions, and public equity. At $95 WTI, the fundamental economics of oil sands production remain positive — Canadian producers are generating cash flow well above sustaining capital requirements. What changes at $80 is the excess free cash flow that was fueling dividend increases and buybacks. Institutional investors with 20-year time horizons are watching the WTI forward curve: at $79 for December 2026 contracts, the market is already pricing a significant reversal of the conflict premium. If that reversal happens faster than the forward curve expects, Canadian energy institutional positions face near-term pressure even as the long-term thesis holds.

SO WHAT — FOR THE SELF-DIRECTED INVESTOR

The institutional money signals this week are unusually consistent: Bank of America abandoning cuts, JPMorgan projecting sustained above-target US inflation, and the Maple 8 watching a WTI forward curve that prices $79 by December. None of these signals requires retail investors to do something dramatic. What they do require is clarity about your time horizon. If you are in XEG for the long-term energy thesis, the forward curve's $79 December price is a risk to watch but not an immediate action item. If you are in VFV expecting the Fed to cut rates and re-expand P/E multiples, Bank of America's revised forecast should update that assumption. And if you are holding cash in a non-registered account watching today's CPI, Tuesday morning belongs in a GIC inside the TFSA regardless of what the number says.
